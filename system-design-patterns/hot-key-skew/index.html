Hot Key / Skew

2026/05/31  ·  System Design Patterns
Contents
Contents

A handful of keys absorb a disproportionate fraction of traffic, so a partitioning scheme that is mathematically balanced on average melts a single shard while the rest of the fleet idles. See glossary for Consistent Hashing, Preference List, Virtual Node (vnode), and Quorum. Core question docs: Ticketmaster, Flash sale, Twitter (celebrity fan-out), YouTube likes counter, Ad click aggregator, Typeahead autocomplete, Distributed cache like Redis.

1. The fundamental hardness

Most partitioning schemes — consistent hashing, range partitioning, hash-mod-N — assume the key distribution is approximately uniform. Under that assumption the load on each shard converges to total_QPS / num_shards, and capacity planning is a multiplication problem. The Law of Large Numbers (LLN) makes everything look smooth in a load test with synthetic data.

Real workloads are Zipfian. Web traffic, search queries, social-graph fan-out, e-commerce SKU pages, ticket inventory rows, ad impressions, viral content — all follow a power-law where the head of the distribution is many orders of magnitude hotter than the median. A workable rule of thumb at the kind of scale these prompts ask about: the top 0.01% of keys absorb on the order of half the requests, and the hottest key is typically 1,000× to 100,000× the median (the very tail of viral spikes — Beyoncé, Super Bowl moment — pushes higher, but treat 10⁶× as an exceptional incident, not a planning constant). Under that distribution one shard sees 0.5 × total_QPS while the other N-1 shards split the remaining half. Doubling cluster size does not help: the hot key still lives on exactly one shard.

The invariant that breaks: per-shard load must stay below per-shard capacity. Consistent hashing preserves key-to-shard stability but is silent about load balance under skew. Virtual nodes (vnodes) smooth out medium-frequency keys but do nothing for a single ultra-hot key — every vnode that owns that key gets the full traffic for it. The result is a hot shard with CPU pegged, queues building, p99 latency climbing, and the dependent caller's connection pool draining. Because failover moves the key to its replica, the replica then melts too — cascading hot-shard failure.

Hot keys are also emergent and time-varying. Beyoncé announces a tour at 10:00 AM and the inventory row that was cold yesterday is the entire system's bottleneck at 10:00:01 AM. The mitigation cannot be a static config; it has to be detection-driven and reactive within seconds.

2. How to recognize it in the interview

Prompt phrases that signal the pattern

  • "Celebrity," "viral," "trending," "Black Friday," "flash sale," "Super Bowl," "Beyoncé seat."
  • "Most-popular X," "leaderboard," "top-K," "global hot list."
  • "Some users have millions of followers," "some posts get a billion views."
  • "One product page gets 90% of traffic during the drop."
  • "Inventory of one ticket / one SKU."
  • "Live event chat" where one stream has 10M concurrent viewers.
  • Any mention of Zipf, power law, long tail, or 80/20.

Architectural smells in stated requirements

  • A single row, key, or counter is the contended resource (e.g., inventory[sku=PS5], likes[video_id=v1]).
  • Read QPS or write QPS quoted in millions but the working-set of "what people actually look at" is small.
  • A per-key strong-consistency requirement layered on top of high QPS (Ticketmaster, payments-against-one-account).
  • Strict ordering or counter semantics on a key that everyone hits.
  • Sharding strategy is "hash by user_id" but the read pattern is "by content_id."

Example "design X" prompts where this is the core hardness

  1. Design Ticketmaster — one seat row, ten million people refreshing; classic write-hot + strong-consistency stack.
  2. Design a flash sale — one SKU, oversell forbidden, 10M shoppers in a 30-second window.
  3. Design Twitter — celebrity with 100M followers; naive fan-out-on-write blows up.
  4. Design YouTube likes counter — one viral video accumulates 1M likes/sec; single-row increment storm.
  5. Design ad click aggregator — one campaign captures most clicks; per-campaign counter is hot.
  6. Design typeahead — "n", "a", "the" prefixes are read millions of times more often than rare prefixes.
  7. Design Twitch chat / live comments — one live stream is the entire system's load.

3. 60-second spoken framing

The first thing I want to call out is that this workload is Zipfian. A small number of keys will take most of the traffic, and consistent hashing alone will not save us — one shard will melt while others idle. So my design has two halves. First, the cold path: standard consistent hashing with virtual nodes, plus per-key Time-To-Live (TTL) caching at the app layer for the long tail. Second, the hot path: I detect hot keys at the edge with a sliding-window counter or a Count-Min Sketch, then apply one or more of: a local first-level (L1) cache with jittered TTL to collapse reads, request coalescing so concurrent misses share one origin fetch, write-side sub-keying so a hot counter becomes N sub-counters summed at read time, and read-side replication of the hot value to every shard. The choice depends on whether the hot key is read-heavy or write-heavy, and whether stale reads are tolerable. I will keep a dedicated hot-key tier as the operational escape hatch, separate from the main store, so a single bad key cannot take down general traffic.

4. Canonical toolkit

Seven techniques. Pick a subset based on read-vs-write skew, consistency budget, and detection latency.

4.1 Local L1 cache with TTL jitter (read-heavy hot key)

What it is. Each application server keeps an in-process Least-Recently-Used (LRU) cache (e.g., Caffeine, Guava, or functools.lru_cache) sitting in front of the distributed cache or store. The TTL has random jitter applied so that expirations don't synchronize across servers.

When to use. Read-heavy hot keys whose value can be stale by seconds (e.g., product page render, profile metadata, top-K list).

Cost. Memory on every app server. Staleness equal to the TTL plus the time to refresh. Cache-coherency lost — invalidation across N servers is best-effort.

When it breaks. When the application requires read-your-write within milliseconds. When the value changes faster than the TTL. When the working set of hot keys is too large to fit in L1.

                ┌───────────────┐
client req ─►   │ App server    │   L1 hit ──► return (sub-ms, zero RPC)
                │  ├─ L1 LRU    │
                │  ├─ Coalescer │   miss ──► single fetch to L2
                │  └─ ────────► │
                └───────────────┘

# Per-server L1 cache with jittered TTL and coalescing.
import asyncio
import random
import time

class HotKeyL1Cache:
    def __init__(self, base_ttl_s=5.0, jitter_s=2.0):
        self.cache = {}                      # key -> (value, expires_at)
        self.inflight = {}                   # key -> asyncio.Future
        self.base_ttl_s = base_ttl_s
        self.jitter_s = jitter_s

    async def get(self, key, loader):
        now = time.monotonic()
        entry = self.cache.get(key)
        if entry and entry[1] > now:
            return entry[0]                  # L1 hit

        # Coalesce: only one in-flight load per key per process.
        fut = self.inflight.get(key)
        if fut is None:
            fut = asyncio.get_event_loop().create_future()
            self.inflight[key] = fut
            try:
                value = await loader(key)    # one RPC to origin
                ttl = self.base_ttl_s + random.uniform(0, self.jitter_s)
                self.cache[key] = (value, now + ttl)
                fut.set_result(value)
            except Exception as e:
                fut.set_exception(e)
            finally:
                self.inflight.pop(key, None)
        return await fut

4.2 Hot-key replication (read-heavy, large fan-out)

What it is. Instead of one shard owning the hot key, replicate it to M shards (M can equal N, the cluster size, in the limit). Reads round-robin across the replica set. Writes fan out to all M replicas synchronously or asynchronously.

When to use. Read-heavy hot keys where the value changes infrequently (top trending list, configuration row, feature flag). Use when L1 caching is insufficient because the working set of hot keys is too large to fit in per-app-server memory or because you need a single tier where every shard owns the key. Note: replication does not by itself give you sub-second freshness — replicas lag by the fan-out time, so this is not a fix for "cannot tolerate seconds of staleness"; combine with synchronous fan-out or quorum reads if that's the constraint.

Cost. Write amplification: one logical write becomes M physical writes in QPS terms, not just storage. If a hot key takes 10K writes/sec and M=64, the cluster does 640K writes/sec for that one key. Storage M× per hot key is usually negligible. The real operational cost is dual code paths in routing and keeping the replica set consistent.

When it breaks. Write-heavy hot keys — write amplification destroys you. Strong-consistency requirements — keeping M replicas in lockstep on every write is essentially a write-quorum problem with M=N.

# Routing layer with hot-key replica fan-out.
class HotKeyRouter:
    def __init__(self, shards, hot_keys_to_replicas):
        self.shards = shards                          # list of shard ids
        self.hot = hot_keys_to_replicas               # key -> list[shard_id]

    def shards_for_read(self, key):
        replicas = self.hot.get(key)
        if replicas:
            # Random pick spreads load evenly.
            return [random.choice(replicas)]
        return [self._hash_to_shard(key)]

    def shards_for_write(self, key):
        return self.hot.get(key) or [self._hash_to_shard(key)]

4.3 Request coalescing / singleflight (read-heavy, expensive origin)

What it is. When N concurrent requests all miss cache for the same key, only one goes to the origin; the others wait on the same future. Implemented as a per-process lock keyed by the hot key. Conceptually the same idea as Go's singleflight package or Java's CompletableFuture deduplication.

When to use. When the upstream fetch is expensive (database query, model inference, downstream service call) and many concurrent misses can collapse to one. Especially during cache-miss-storm scenarios after a key expires.

Cost. Adds latency to the (N-1) waiting requests — they pay the fetch time, not the cache-hit time. Single point of failure: if the leader fetch hangs, all coalesced requests time out together.

When it breaks. When the upstream is intermittently slow — one slow leader fetch blocks everyone. Mitigate with per-request timeout shorter than the leader fetch timeout, so callers can fail fast and retry independently.

4.4 Sub-keying / counter sharding (write-heavy hot key)

What it is. Replace one logical hot key with K sub-keys hashed by the writer (e.g., counter[video=v1] becomes counter[video=v1#bucket=0..63]). Each sub-key is independently incremented. Reads sum across the K sub-keys, optionally with a small cache to avoid the fan-in cost on every read.

When to use. Write-heavy hot keys where reads are infrequent or tolerant of a small fan-in: like counters, leaderboards, ad impression buckets, like counts, view counts. Pairs perfectly with real-time-aggregation.

Cost. Read amplification: one logical read becomes K physical reads (or K reads against a periodic rollup). Operational: K must be chosen carefully — too small and you still have a hot sub-key; too large and reads get expensive.

When it breaks. Strong-consistency reads (the K sub-keys can be in-flight at different times, so the sum is approximate by O(K × per-bucket-batch-latency)). Read-modify-write semantics (e.g., "increment if value < cap") — needs a coordinator, see strong-consistency-under-contention.

                                          writer_id mod 4 picks the bucket
  ┌─────────────────┐                     ┌──────┐ ┌──────┐ ┌──────┐ ┌──────┐
  │ counter[v1] = N │  ◄── 1M w/s          │ b0=… │ │ b1=… │ │ b2=… │ │ b3=… │
  └─────────────────┘                     └──────┘ └──────┘ └──────┘ └──────┘

# Write-side sub-keying for a counter.
class ShardedCounter:
    def __init__(self, kv, num_buckets=64):
        self.kv = kv
        self.k = num_buckets

    def incr(self, key, writer_hint):
        bucket = hash(writer_hint) % self.k
        self.kv.incr(f"{key}#{bucket}")

    def value(self, key):
        # Fan-in read; cache the result with short TTL if reads are hot too.
        return sum(self.kv.get(f"{key}#{i}", 0) for i in range(self.k))

4.5 Client-side hot-key detection and fan-out

What it is. Each client (app server, edge proxy) maintains a small sketch — a Count-Min Sketch with a sliding window, or a top-K stream summary — of per-key request rates. When a key crosses a threshold (e.g., > 1% of total QPS or > 1k QPS absolute), the client locally flips that key into "hot mode": route through the L1 cache, coalesce, fan out to replica set, and emit a metric so operators can promote the key to the dedicated hot tier.

When to use. As the front-end of any of the above techniques — you cannot apply them to every key (memory cost), so detection narrows the set. Essential when hot keys are emergent (Beyoncé tour announcement).

Cost. Sketch memory per client (small, KB range). False positives waste hot-path budget; false negatives let a hot key slip through.

When it breaks. Highly bursty traffic where the hot key changes within the sketch window. Coordinated misdetection across clients can cause synchronized cache rebuilds.

# Sliding-window Count-Min Sketch for hot-key detection.
import collections
import time

class HotKeyDetector:
    def __init__(self, window_s=10, hot_threshold_qps=1000):
        self.window_s = window_s
        self.threshold = hot_threshold_qps
        self.buckets = collections.deque()  # (ts, key_counts)

    def observe(self, key):
        now = time.time()
        if not self.buckets or self.buckets[-1][0] != int(now):
            self.buckets.append((int(now), collections.Counter()))
        self.buckets[-1][1][key] += 1
        while self.buckets and self.buckets[0][0] < now - self.window_s:
            self.buckets.popleft()

    def is_hot(self, key):
        total = sum(b[1].get(key, 0) for b in self.buckets)
        return (total / self.window_s) >= self.threshold

4.6 Dedicated hot-key tier (operational escape hatch)

What it is. A separate, in-memory, read-optimized store (Redis cluster, Memcached, custom service) that hot keys are promoted into — either automatically when detection fires, or manually by an operator before a known event. The main store still holds the truth; the hot tier is a read replica with explicit invalidation on writes. Some designs make the hot tier write-through; others keep it read-only and async-refreshed.

When to use. Anchor strategy for known hot events (flash sale, ticket onsale, sports finals). Also as the operational landing spot when detection promotes a key.

Cost. Two stores to operate, two invalidation paths to keep consistent. Cold reads after promotion incur a one-shot main-store hit.

When it breaks. When hot keys are write-heavy and consistency-critical — the dual-write is hard. When promotion is slow relative to the traffic spike (Beyoncé announcement: spike arrives in seconds).

4.7 Edge / Content Delivery Network (CDN) caching for hot read keys

What it is. Push hot read keys to the CDN edge with short TTL and stale-while-revalidate semantics. The edge fans out to thousands of points of presence (PoP), each serving a slice of the world's traffic. Only the origin sees ~1 / num_edges of the traffic, gated by TTL refresh.

When to use. Public, cacheable HTTP responses for the hot key: product detail pages, public profile pages, viral content. Especially for read-heavy with seconds-tolerable staleness.

Cost. Per-user personalization defeats it. Invalidation is slow (CDN purge can take minutes). Cache-key engineering (which query params and headers vary) is non-trivial.

When it breaks. Logged-in personalized responses. Real-time freshness requirements. Cache-bypass attacks where adversaries vary cache-key components to force origin pass-through (see cache-stampede-read-amplification).

4.8 Per-key admission control (operational backstop)

What it is. Independent of all the above, install a per-key token-bucket rate limiter at the edge or load balancer. The bucket fills at a rate slightly above the hot tier's measured capacity for that key. Excess is shed (return 429 / queue / show a waiting room).

When to use. As the always-on safety net. The other techniques optimize capacity; admission control protects availability when capacity is exceeded faster than mitigation can react.

Cost. Visible user impact: shed users see errors or queues. Operational complexity: per-key limits must be tuned and updated as the hot set evolves.

When it breaks. When users retry aggressively — shed load returns as retry storms. Mitigate with Retry-After headers and exponential backoff at the client. See backpressure-flow-control.

# Per-key token bucket; admit only what the hot tier can handle.
import time

class PerKeyAdmission:
    def __init__(self, capacity_per_key):
        self.cap = capacity_per_key          # key -> tokens/sec
        self.state = {}                      # key -> (tokens, last_refill)

    def admit(self, key):
        rate = self.cap.get(key, float("inf"))
        tokens, last = self.state.get(key, (rate, time.monotonic()))
        now = time.monotonic()
        tokens = min(rate, tokens + (now - last) * rate)
        if tokens >= 1:
            self.state[key] = (tokens - 1, now)
            return True
        self.state[key] = (tokens, now)
        return False                          # shed

5. Worked example: naive → broken → fixed

Scenario. YouTube-style likes counter. 1 million videos in catalog, but on a Tuesday afternoon one music video drops and absorbs the spike.

Workload.

  • Total write QPS across all videos: 200K likes/sec.
  • Distribution: top video takes 60% of writes = 120K likes/sec on one key.
  • Reads (rendering "like count" on every page view): 2M reads/sec total, top video takes 50% = 1M reads/sec on one key.
  • Backing store: 32 shards of a Key-Value (KV) store; each shard can handle 30K ops/sec sustained.

Naive design

  • One row per video: likes[video_id] = count.
  • Consistent hashing by video_id over 32 shards.
  • Writes: INCR likes[video_id].
  • Reads: GET likes[video_id].

Failure mode

  • The hot video lands on one shard. That shard sees 120K writes/sec + 1M reads/sec = 1.12M ops/sec against a 30K capacity.
  • Result: shard CPU at 100%, queue depth growing, p99 latency from 5 ms to 30 sec, then timeouts.
  • Failover does not help — the replica becomes the new hot shard.
  • The other 31 shards are idle at ~3K ops/sec each.
  • Per-shard utilization: 1 shard at 37× capacity, 31 shards at 10% capacity.

Fixed design

Three-layer mitigation:

  1. Sub-keyed write counter. likes[v#0..63], one of K=64 sub-keys chosen by hash(user_id) % 64. 120K writes/sec divided by 64 = ~1.9K writes/sec per sub-key. Sub-keys are themselves consistent-hashed across 32 shards, so the 64 sub-keys distribute approximately evenly. No single shard sees more than ~4K writes/sec from the hot video.
  2. Read-side aggregation with cache. A background roll-up job sums likes[v#0..63] every 1 second and writes the total to likes_total[v] with TTL=2 seconds. Reads hit the total, which is now in a regular cache.
  3. L1 cache + edge CDN for reads. The total is also cacheable at the CDN with TTL=1 second and stale-while-revalidate=5 seconds. Of 1M reads/sec, ~99% are served at edge. Origin sees ~10K reads/sec for this key — fits comfortably.

Numbers after fix

Layer Load on hot key Capacity headroom
CDN edge 1M reads/sec Designed for it; horizontally trivial.
Origin reads ~10K reads/sec 30K/shard; 33% utilization.
Origin writes (per sub-key) ~1.9K writes/sec 6% utilization per shard.
Worst single shard ~6K total ops/sec 20% utilization.
Other 31 shards unchanged ~3K ops/sec, ~10%.

Tradeoff incurred: like count is stale by up to ~1 second. Acceptable for the product.

What changed conceptually

  • Writes were spread (sub-keying), not concentrated. The hot row dissolves into 64 lukewarm rows.
  • Reads were aggregated once, served many times (roll-up + CDN). The N×M fan-in of "read 64 sub-keys per request" is paid 1×/second instead of 1×/request.
  • The detection layer is not in the hot path — sub-keying is unconditional for likes. The detector only matters for the next unknown hot pattern.

Why not just "cache the total"?

Reads were the cheap fix; the storm was on the write side. Caching alone leaves 120K writes/sec hammering one shard. The interview signal is to address both axes — read amplification and write amplification — with separate, layered techniques.

6. Variants and how to choose

Hot-key problems split along two axes: read vs write and consistency tolerance. Pick the toolkit based on the quadrant.

Variant Hot key shape Consistency budget Primary technique Secondary Anti-pattern
Hot read, eventually consistent Product page, profile, viral video metadata Seconds CDN/edge + L1 cache + jittered TTL Coalescing on miss Hitting origin for every read
Hot read, strongly consistent Live inventory display Sub-second Hot-key replication (M=N) + write fan-out Detection-driven promotion Naive single-shard ownership
Hot write, no aggregation Per-key audit log append Eventual Sub-keying / counter sharding Async batching One row, one writer
Hot write, with read aggregation Likes / views / clicks counter 1-5 sec Sub-keying + periodic roll-up L1 on roll-up read Distributed lock on single counter
Hot read-modify-write, strict Ticket seat reservation, stock decrement Strong + ordered Single-writer queue per key + coordinated state machine Optimistic Concurrency Control (OCC) Spreading the write — breaks the invariant

The fifth row is the trap. Sub-keying a counter that has the invariant "must not go negative" silently breaks correctness, because no single sub-key knows the total. For that variant, see strong-consistency-under-contention and coordination-exactly-one-execution.

On-call incident playbook (hot-key cascade)

  1. Identify. topk_by_qps on the hot shard's metrics. Confirm one key dominates.
  2. Isolate. Apply per-key rate limit at the edge (drop or shed beyond 2× capacity / num_replicas). Other traffic continues.
  3. Promote. Move the hot key to the hot tier. If write-heavy, enable sub-keying for that key.
  4. Verify. Per-shard CPU drops; aggregate error rate falls.
  5. Postmortem inputs. Was the key in the detector's top-K but not promoted? Why? Detection lag? Threshold? Was the hot tier overprovisioned for the spike?

8. Combinations with other patterns

Hot key / skew rarely appears alone. The most common stacks:

  • cache-stampede-read-amplification — A hot key whose Time-To-Live (TTL) expires causes simultaneous origin fetches from every cache node. Singleflight at every layer + jittered TTL + stale-while-revalidate is the combined defense.
  • fan-out-write-amplification — A celebrity's tweet has 100M followers; the fan-out itself is the hot-key problem on the writer side. The mitigation is the dual of write fan-out: pull-on-read for celebrity tweets, plus the toolkit here for the read side.
  • strong-consistency-under-contention — Ticketmaster seat row: hot key + the invariant "one seat sells once." Sub-keying breaks the invariant; the right tool is single-writer queue or optimistic locking, plus rate-limiting upstream.
  • real-time-aggregation — Counter sub-keying produces N sub-counters that must be aggregated; the aggregation tier itself becomes a hot path during spikes.
  • backpressure-flow-control — When mitigation can't be deployed fast enough, the only way to keep the cluster up is to shed load on the hot key specifically rather than the whole cluster.
  • coordination-exactly-one-execution — Hot key + "must execute exactly once" (flash-sale checkout) needs the coordination pattern plus per-key admission control.
  • idempotency-exactly-once — Hot writes amplify retries; idempotency keys must be enforced cheaply (Bloom filter at the front, deduplication DB behind).

9. Likely interviewer probes

  1. "Show me the math: how skewed is your workload?" Strong answer: name the distribution (Zipf with parameter ~1.0), give the rule of thumb (top 0.01% takes ~50%), pick a concrete hot key (one video, one product, one seat row), compute the shard load assuming uniform partitioning. If the hottest single key takes fraction f of total QPS and per-shard capacity is provisioned at total_QPS / N, the hot shard runs at f × N times its provisioned capacity. For f = 0.5 and N = 32, that's 16× over capacity — explaining concretely why "add more shards" doesn't help.

  2. "How do you detect hot keys at runtime?" Strong answer: sliding-window Count-Min Sketch (CMS) at every app server. Sketch dimensions sized for false positive rate < 1%, window 10 seconds, top-K extracted every second. Server-side detector on the shard itself as backstop. Detector emits to a control plane that decides promotion. Threshold has hysteresis: hot when sustained > 1k QPS for 5 seconds, demote when below 100 QPS for 60 seconds.

  3. "What if the hot key is write-heavy and requires strong consistency, like a flash sale inventory row?" Strong answer: you cannot sub-key — breaks the invariant. Two patterns work. (a) Single-writer queue: route all writes for that key to one writer process which holds a lease; writer processes a batch with one transactional commit per batch. (b) Optimistic concurrency: each writer reads version, attempts conditional write; retry on conflict. Both shape: write rate becomes serialized, so capacity is 1 / commit_latency. Realistically that's ~10K-30K tx/sec per partition on a good transactional store; batching commits (commit N decrements as one transaction) pushes throughput up by the batch size, at the cost of per-request latency. To go higher than batched single-writer can sustain, the inventory itself must be partitioned (e.g., reserve 100 seats per partition, then a partition becomes the hot row at smaller scale).

  4. "How does this interact with consistent hashing and virtual nodes?" Strong answer: vnodes spread medium-frequency keys evenly. They do not help a single ultra-hot key — the key still hashes to exactly one (vnode, shard) pair. Hot-key replication breaks the vnode invariant by writing to multiple positions on the ring, requiring the router to know which keys are special.

  5. "What's the cost of hot-key replication at M=N?" Strong answer: write amplification N× in QPS, not just storage — a 10K writes/sec hot key on a 64-shard cluster becomes 640K cluster-wide writes for that one key, so this technique only pays off when the key is read-heavy (read:write ratio ≫ N). Storage N× per hot key is negligible if hot keys are few (100 keys × 64 shards = 6,400 extra rows). Fan-out latency for writes equals the slowest replica. Operational cost on top: dual code paths in the router, replication lag monitoring, divergence detection. The technique is the wrong choice for a write-heavy hot counter; reach for sub-keying instead.

  6. "How do you handle a hot key that is also large (multi-MB value)?" Strong answer: don't replicate the full value to every shard. Chunk the value, hot-key the index, distribute the chunks. Or store the value once in object storage with a CDN in front, and hot-key only the metadata pointer. Or shape the API so the value is materialized client-side from cacheable pieces.

  7. "Walk me through how Beyoncé announces a tour at 10:00:00 and the system stays up." Strong answer: at 09:58 detection sketch is cold for the new event. At 10:00:00 announce drops; QPS on the event row goes from 0 to 500k/sec in 5 seconds. CMS detects it at 10:00:02, control plane promotes by 10:00:03, hot tier serves from 10:00:04. The 4-second gap is the design's actual exposure — during that window the origin shard is over capacity, so survival depends on three things, not the promotion itself: (a) per-key admission control at the edge capping origin load at, say, 5-10× normal — most requests get a 429 or a queue page; (b) singleflight at every cache layer to collapse concurrent misses on the same key; (c) graceful degradation — return a queue page or stale snapshot rather than a hard error. Honest framing: with these in place, the cluster stays up and most users see a holding page for 4 seconds, not a successful low-latency read. If the event is known in advance, pre-announcement static promotion eliminates the gap entirely; that's the right design when you can, and admission control is the fallback when you can't.

10. Common traps → stronger moves

Trap Stronger move
"Consistent hashing solves all sharding." Consistent hashing balances keys assuming uniform key access. Under Zipf, one key is the load. Vnodes help medium-frequency, not the head.
Sub-keying a counter that has a hard upper bound (inventory). Single-writer queue or optimistic-concurrency control; sub-keying violates the invariant.
Static hot-key list configured once. Runtime detection (CMS or top-K sketch) + control plane that promotes/demotes dynamically.
Global Time-To-Live (TTL) on cache, no jitter. TTL with uniform jitter; stale-while-revalidate; singleflight on miss.
Replicate all keys to all shards "just in case." Only replicate the detected hot set; ratio is asymmetric (head of distribution is tiny).
Rely on the main store's cache to absorb the hot key. Dedicated hot tier — main store cache is a shared resource and gets evicted by other traffic.
Edge cache without invalidation strategy. Soft TTL + purge API; design the cache key so partial invalidation is possible.
Per-cluster rate limit when one key is hot. Per-key rate limit; preserve cold-path traffic.
"Just add more shards." More shards do not help — the hot key still lives on one. Scale the hot-key tier independently.
Treat hot-key handling as a steady-state config. Treat it as a control loop with detection, decision, action, and rollback.
Singleflight only at the application layer. Singleflight at every cache layer (L1, L2, edge); cache stampedes appear at the layer below the one with deduplication.
