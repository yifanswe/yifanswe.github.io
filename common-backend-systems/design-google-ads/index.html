1. Basic Knowledge And Solution

What This Question Is Testing

Google Ads is real-time ad auction at planet scale: every search query and page view triggers an auction among eligible advertisers to determine which ad to serve. The hard part is sub-100ms auction latency, budget enforcement under burst traffic, click attribution, billing correctness, and ad quality / safety.

The invariant is this:

For every ad request, the system runs a deterministic auction among eligible advertisers, selects winners per slot, serves ads within latency budget, charges advertisers per click/impression deterministically, enforces budget caps before serving ads that exceed budget, and produces an auditable trail of every ad served and the auction that produced it.

At ICT5 / L6+ level, the interviewer is testing whether you can design real-time auction with millisecond latency, ML-based bid scoring and click prediction, budget enforcement under burst, click attribution against fraud, and billing reconciliation. This combines Design an Ad Click Aggregator with the auction front-end.

60-Second Interview Framing

Spoken version:

I would design Google Ads as a real-time auction system. For every ad slot, an ad server queries eligible advertisers, runs a Vickrey or generalized second-price auction with quality scores, returns the winning ad. Budgets are enforced via fast counters with predictive cutoff. Clicks are captured and attributed via the ad click aggregator. Billing reconciles daily. The interesting decisions are auction mechanics, budget enforcement under burst, click fraud, and ML scoring.

Requirements To Clarify First

Functional requirements:

  • Advertisers create campaigns with budget, targeting, bid.
  • For each ad slot (search results, display, video), run auction.
  • Select winning ad(s), serve.
  • Capture clicks; attribute to ad.
  • Charge advertiser per CPC, CPM, or CPA model.
  • Budget cap enforcement.
  • Reporting dashboards for advertisers.
  • Fraud filtering.

Non-functional requirements:

  • Auction latency under 100ms.
  • Trillions of impressions/day.
  • No overspend per campaign budget.
  • Click attribution accurate.
  • Auditability of every auction and bill.
  • Multi-region.

Out of scope unless asked:

  • Ad creative editing tools.
  • Display ad network details.
  • Brand safety beyond basic filtering.

Auction Flow

For each ad slot:

  1. Ad server receives request with context (query, user, page).
  2. Candidate retrieval: targeting index returns eligible ads matching context (~thousands).
  3. Quality scoring: ML model predicts CTR (or expected value) for each candidate ad given context.
  4. Bid + quality: compute effective_bid = bid * quality_score.
  5. Auction: generalized second-price (GSP) — rank by effective_bid; top K win positions; each pays the next-bid's price plus a cent.
  6. Budget check: for each winner, confirm campaign has budget; if not, drop.
  7. Return ads to ad server; impressions logged.
  8. Async pipeline: record auction details for audit, billing, and reporting.

Latency budget: tens of ms total.

Interview phrase:

The auction is a deterministic ranking by effective_bid (bid × quality). GSP charges each winner the next bidder's effective bid divided by their quality — incentive-compatible enough in practice. The hard part is doing this in milliseconds for millions of QPS.

Quality Score And CTR Prediction

Quality score is per-ad-context-user. Combines: - Predicted CTR (most important). - Ad relevance to query. - Landing page experience.

CTR prediction is a deep learning model trained on historical impressions and clicks.

Features: - Ad features (text, image, advertiser, history). - User features (history, demographics, recent queries). - Context features (query, time, device).

Model serves on the auction path with strict latency. Pre-computed candidate features; online features per request.

Budget Enforcement

Each campaign has daily and total budgets. The system must not overspend.

Same challenge as ad click aggregator. Mitigations:

  • Real-time counter per campaign (sub-counter sharded).
  • Predictive cutoff: stop serving ads when budget is 95% spent.
  • Pacing: smooth spend across the day; if 50% through the day, don't have 80% of budget spent.
  • Per-region budget allocation for very large campaigns.

When a campaign exhausts budget, ad server skips it in auction. Async aggregator catches up to authoritative spend.

Click Capture And Attribution

User clicks ad:

  1. Browser navigates to a Google redirect URL containing signed click_id.
  2. Click handler validates signature, records click.
  3. Attribution: this click corresponds to which ad, which auction, which user.
  4. Redirect to advertiser's destination URL.
  5. Capture stored for billing pipeline.

Idempotency: same click_id appearing twice (refresh, retry) records only once.

Fraud Detection

Async pipeline. Same as ad click aggregator:

  • Bot patterns (high click rate per IP/user, suspicious clusters).
  • Click farms.
  • Competitor click bombing.
  • Self-clicking advertisers.

ML scoring per click. Suspicious clicks excluded from billing.

Billing And Reconciliation

Hot path: counter incremented per validated click (advertiser charged). Cold path: nightly reconciliation against fraud-filtered click log. Adjust if drift.

Authoritative: cold path. Hot path is approximate for budget enforcement.

Pacing

Without pacing, large campaigns would exhaust budget early in the day. Pacing controls:

  • Track time-of-day vs budget-used ratio.
  • If ratio off, dampen bids or skip auctions for that campaign.
  • Goal: spend evenly across day.

  • Auction per region for low latency.
  • Campaign metadata replicated globally.
  • Budget per region with cross-region aggregation.
  • Click capture region-local.
  • Billing centralized.

Failure Modes And Mitigations

Failure mode Mitigation Signal to monitor
Auction latency spike. Strict timeout; degrade to fewer candidates. Auction p99.
Budget overspend. Predictive cutoff; pacing. Over-budget %.
Click fraud. Async filter; reversal. Fraud rate.
Ad serving outage. Fallback to lower-quality ads or no ads. Serving error rate.
Counter saturation. Sub-counter sharding. Per-counter QPS.
Cross-region clock skew. Bounded; per-region allocation. Skew detection.
Campaign edits not propagating. Versioned; alert on lag. Propagation lag.
Bad ML model. Canary; auto-rollback. CTR delta.
Brand safety incident. Blocklists; manual review. Reported incidents.
Reconciliation drift. Daily compare; alerts. Drift %.

  • Fraud detection rate.

  • Auctions per second.

  • CTR per ad.

Tradeoffs To Call Out

  • Tighter auction latency vs more candidates considered.
  • Strict budget cutoff vs slight over-serve.
  • Aggressive fraud filter vs false positives (lost revenue).
  • Pacing controls vs advertiser preference for "spend ASAP."
  • More ML features improve CTR prediction but cost latency.
  • Per-region budget vs global accuracy.

2. Simulated Interview

Interviewer: Design Google Ads.

Candidate: Real-time auction system. Every impression triggers: candidate retrieval from targeting index, ML scoring for CTR, GSP auction ranking by bid × quality, budget check, return ads. Click capture is idempotent; fraud filtering async; billing reconciled daily. The invariant is no overspend per budget, fair auction outcome, sub-100ms latency.

Interviewer: Walk me through an auction.

Candidate: Ad server receives request with context (query, user, geo). Targeting index returns eligible ads — thousands. ML scoring model predicts CTR for each candidate-context. Compute effective_bid = bid × quality_score. Generalized Second-Price auction: rank by effective_bid; top K win positions. Each winner pays the next-bidder's effective bid divided by their quality. Budget check for each winner: confirm campaign has budget; if not, drop. Return ads. Log auction. All within tens of ms.

Interviewer: Why GSP not first-price?

Candidate: Incentive properties. In first-price, bidders shade their bids strategically. GSP makes truthful bidding closer to optimal (though not strictly truthful like Vickrey for single-slot). VCG is fully truthful but rarely used because of complexity. GSP is the industry standard for ad auctions because it balances simplicity and incentive compatibility.

Interviewer: Budget enforcement?

Candidate: Real-time counter per campaign. When ad is served, increment a sub-counter (sharded for hot campaigns). When user clicks, increment with the click's billed amount. Predictive cutoff: stop serving when counter reaches 95% of budget, accounting for in-flight impressions. Pacing: track time-of-day vs spend ratio; dampen bids if ahead of schedule.

Interviewer: Click capture?

Candidate: Browser clicks an ad → navigates to a Google redirect URL with signed click_id. Click handler validates signature, records click. Idempotent on click_id (same click_id appearing twice — refresh — records once). Redirect to advertiser destination. Click event flows to billing pipeline.

Interviewer: Click fraud?

Candidate: Async pipeline. Same as ad click aggregator design. ML scoring per click — high rate per IP/user, bot patterns, competitor click bombing, advertiser self-clicks. Suspicious clicks excluded from billing. Retroactive reversal if detected after billing.

Interviewer: CTR prediction model — how does it serve sub-50ms?

Candidate: Deep model with feature store. Pre-computed candidate features (ad features, historical CTR). Online features per request (query, user, time). Model serves on GPU/CPU inference workers. Strict latency budget; fall back to cached predictions if model times out. Continuous training pipeline updates the model with fresh data.

Interviewer: A campaign has $1M daily budget and gets a sudden traffic spike.

Candidate: Sub-counter sharding handles write rate. Predictive cutoff prevents significant overshoot. Pacing dampens bid aggressiveness if spending too fast. If the spike persists, the campaign exhausts budget mid-day and stops serving for the rest. Smart bidding strategies may auto-raise the budget if the advertiser opted in.

Interviewer: Reconciliation?

Candidate: Hot path increments counter in real-time for budget enforcement. Cold path runs nightly: read all clicks for the day, apply fraud filtering, sum, compare to hot-path counter. Cold path is authoritative; hot path is operational. Drift triggers alerts. Advertiser dashboards show authoritative numbers after reconciliation.

Interviewer: Multi-region?

Candidate: Auction per region — lower latency for users globally. Campaign metadata replicated globally. Budget per region with cross-region aggregation; very large global campaigns split budget across regions to avoid hot-counter contention. Click capture region-local. Billing centralized after reconciliation.

Interviewer: Brand safety?

Candidate: Multiple layers. Pre-emptive: review ad creatives at submission (auto + human). Block-list keywords and contexts. Real-time: at auction, exclude ads from sensitive contexts (e.g., breaking news, hate speech). Reactive: monitor for reported incidents, takedown bad ads, account suspension.

Interviewer: Summarize.

Candidate: Real-time auction with candidate retrieval + ML scoring + GSP auction + budget check, all in tens of ms. Click capture is idempotent; fraud filtering async. Budget enforcement via real-time counters with predictive cutoff and pacing. Daily reconciliation against authoritative click log. ML drives CTR prediction. Multi-region per-auction with central billing. The hardest correctness problem is no overspend; the hardest performance problem is sub-100ms auction.
