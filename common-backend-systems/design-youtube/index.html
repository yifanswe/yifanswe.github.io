1. Basic Knowledge And Solution

What This Question Is Testing

YouTube is video at planet scale. The hard part is upload of hours of video per second, transcoding into many formats, durable storage of exabytes, delivery to billions of viewers with adaptive bitrate, and recommendation/ranking that makes content discoverable.

The invariant is this:

Every uploaded video is durably stored, transcoded into appropriate formats, served via CDN with adaptive bitrate to match viewer bandwidth and device, and remains available; engagement counters, recommendations, and search converge to consistent state; deletes and visibility changes propagate within seconds.

At ICT5 / L6+ level, the interviewer is testing whether you can design the upload + transcode pipeline, CDN strategy for petabytes of egress, adaptive bitrate streaming, recommendation system architecture, and reason about hot-content surges (viral videos).

60-Second Interview Framing

Spoken version:

I would design YouTube as four major systems: an upload + async transcoding pipeline, a CDN-backed video delivery layer with adaptive bitrate streaming, a feed and search service for discovery, and a recommendation pipeline that drives the homepage and related videos. The interesting decisions are transcoding for many formats, CDN tiering for cost, adaptive bitrate via HLS/DASH, recommendation freshness, and viral hot-content handling.

Requirements To Clarify First

Functional requirements:

  • Creators upload videos (any length, common formats).
  • Viewers watch on web, mobile, smart TV with adaptive quality.
  • Search, recommendations, subscriptions.
  • Comments, likes, dislikes (or just likes now).
  • Channels with subscribe button.
  • Live streaming (out of scope unless asked).
  • Monetization, ads (out of scope mostly).

Non-functional requirements:

  • 500+ hours of video uploaded per minute.
  • Billions of views per day.
  • Sub-second video start (TTFB to first frame).
  • Petabytes of storage.
  • 4K and beyond support.
  • Multi-region delivery.

Out of scope unless asked:

  • Live streaming pipeline.
  • Ad serving and auctions.
  • Monetization split.
  • Copyright detection (Content ID).

Upload And Transcode

Upload:

  1. Creator client requests upload URL; gets signed URL + video_id.
  2. Client uploads chunks directly to object storage (resumable).
  3. Client calls complete.
  4. Upload service marks video status=processing; enqueues transcode job.

Transcode:

  1. Job picked up by transcode worker.
  2. Original split into time-based segments (e.g., 6-second).
  3. Parallel transcode of segments to each target resolution and codec.
  4. Generate ABR manifest (HLS .m3u8 or DASH .mpd) listing segments per resolution.
  5. Generate thumbnails (multiple frames).
  6. Write derivatives to derivative store.
  7. Update video status=ready once at least one usable derivative complete.
  8. Higher resolutions can land later.

Cost optimization:

  • Spot instances for transcode.
  • Skip rare resolutions (4K) until requested.
  • Re-transcode to new codecs (AV1) as economics shift.
  • Tier original to cold storage after derivatives stable.

Interview phrase:

Transcoding is where YouTube spends a fortune. We parallelize per-segment, prioritize quality levels by expected demand, and use spot/preemptible capacity aggressively.

Adaptive Bitrate Streaming (HLS / DASH)

The viewer's player picks the best resolution based on current bandwidth, buffer, and device capability.

  • Manifest lists segments at multiple resolutions.
  • Player downloads segments one at a time, ~6 seconds long.
  • Between segments, player measures network and may switch resolution.
  • Buffer ahead 30-60 seconds; rebuffer if drained.

Server side:

  • All segments served from CDN.
  • Manifest is small; fetched once and refreshed for live content.

CDN Strategy

Egress is the dominant cost. Hit ratio is the key metric.

Multi-tier:

  • Device cache: browser/app holds recently watched.
  • Edge POPs: hundreds globally, geographically close to viewers.
  • Regional caches: larger, serve cache misses from edge.
  • Origin: derivative store; expensive to hit.

Cache strategy:

  • Long TTLs because derivatives are immutable.
  • LRU eviction; popular videos stay hot.
  • Pre-warm POPs for predicted viral content.
  • Cache partitioning by video_id segment to scale.

Hit ratio target: 90%+. The 10% miss is the budget for origin bandwidth.

Adaptive prefetch: player downloads next segment while user watches current.

Recommendations

Two-stage:

Offline (daily / hourly): - Build candidate sets per user: "similar to recently watched," "trending in your region," "from your subscriptions." - Store as pre-computed lists.

Online (per request): - Ranker scores candidates with timeout (~100 ms). - Features: viewer history, engagement, freshness, video performance. - Returns ordered list.

Architecture:

  • Watch events feed feature stores.
  • Daily batch job updates embeddings.
  • Real-time signals (view, like) update online features.

Search

Inverted index of video metadata (title, description, channel) + transcripts.

  • Index sharded by token hash.
  • Score by relevance, popularity, freshness, channel authority.
  • Eventual consistency: new video indexed within minutes.

Engagement Counters

Same pattern as YouTube likes design (separate doc). Per-video sub-counter sharding for viral videos; periodic recount for correctness.

Multi-Region Strategy

  • Object storage geo-replicated for derivatives; original may stay in upload region.
  • CDN edges everywhere.
  • Video metadata replicated; per-video home region.
  • Recommendation features regional; cross-region replication for global trending.

Failure Modes And Mitigations

Failure mode Mitigation Signal to monitor
Transcode failure. Retry; degrade to fewer resolutions. Transcode failure rate.
Viral video CDN miss storm. Pre-warm edges; multi-tier cache. Origin egress; hit ratio.
Derivative corruption. Checksum on read; rebuild from original. Hash mismatch.
Origin overloaded. Throttle; tier-up serving; request coalescing. Origin QPS.
Slow viewer device. ABR drops to lower resolution. Resolution distribution.
Search lag for hot upload. Async index; pre-index for known channels. Index lag.
Recommender slow. Strict timeout; fallback to trending. Ranker timeout rate.
Storage cost growth. Cold-tier originals; delete derivatives rarely accessed. Storage growth rate.
Deleted video still served. CDN purge + cache invalidation. Stale-serve count.
Geographic restriction violated. Edge-level rule enforcement. Geo-violation count.

Operational Metrics

Correctness:

  • Stale serve after delete.
  • Transcode failures.
  • Engagement counter drift.

Capacity:

  • Egress bandwidth by region.
  • CDN hit ratio.
  • Origin QPS.
  • Transcode queue depth.

Business:

  • Watch time.
  • Engagement per video.
  • Recommendation CTR.

Tradeoffs To Call Out

  • Pre-generating all resolutions vs on-demand: pre-gen saves first-view latency, on-demand saves storage.
  • Strict CDN TTLs vs invalidation flexibility.
  • Aggressive ABR resolution selection vs smooth playback.
  • Recommendation freshness vs compute cost.
  • Search index size vs query latency.
  • Pre-warming edges for predicted viral content vs missed predictions.

YouTube is Instagram for video plus a recommendation engine plus a search engine. The dominant cost is egress; CDN strategy is half the design. Transcoding parallelizes per-segment. 2. Simulated Interview

Interviewer: Design YouTube.

Candidate: I will design four major systems: upload + transcode pipeline, multi-tier CDN for delivery, a metadata + search service, and a recommendation pipeline. The invariant is durable storage of every upload, transcoded into formats for ABR playback, served at sub-second start latency, with engagement counters and search converging within minutes.

Interviewer: Walk me through upload.

Candidate: Creator client requests upload URL and video_id. Uploads chunks directly to object storage via signed URL — resumable, can survive client network blips. On complete, video marked processing. Transcode job enqueued. Worker splits original into 6-second segments, parallel-transcodes each to multiple resolutions (144p through 4K) and codecs (H.264, VP9, AV1). Generates HLS and DASH manifests. Once at least one usable resolution is ready, video marked ready and publishable.

Interviewer: Why per-segment transcode?

Candidate: Parallelism. A 1-hour video would take an hour to transcode sequentially. Split into 600 6-second segments, transcode in parallel across 600 workers, done in minutes. The output is also natively suitable for HLS/DASH segmented delivery.

Interviewer: Adaptive bitrate — how does the player decide?

Candidate: Player downloads the manifest, then fetches segments one at a time. Between segments, it measures download time vs segment duration to estimate available bandwidth. It checks current buffer level. Picks the highest resolution it can sustain without buffer underrun. If conditions degrade, drops to lower resolution; if they improve, climbs. The server just serves segments; intelligence is in the client.

Interviewer: CDN strategy?

Candidate: Multi-tier. Edge POPs are the closest layer to viewers — hundreds globally. Regional caches sit behind edges. Origin is the derivative store. Hit ratio target above 90% because origin egress is expensive. Long cache TTLs because derivatives are immutable. Pre-warm edges for predicted viral content. Request coalescing on cache miss to avoid stampede. Each video's segments are sharded by id so different segments may serve from different cache nodes.

Interviewer: A video goes viral — 100,000 concurrent views in a region.

Candidate: Edge POP cache absorbs most. The first miss for each segment goes to regional cache, then origin. Request coalescing on the edge: 100 concurrent misses for the same segment become one upstream request, fanned out to all waiting clients on response. We monitor egress and pre-warm additional edges. Recommendation engine may surface to even more viewers, compounding load — we backpressure the recommender if origin is saturated.

Interviewer: Recommendations?

Candidate: Two-stage. Offline: daily batch builds candidate sets per user from collaborative filtering, similar-content embeddings, subscriptions, trending. Each user has a stored candidate list. Online: when user requests homepage, fetch candidates, send to ranker with viewer context. Ranker scores with features (watch history, engagement, freshness, video performance). Strict 100ms timeout; fallback to recent trending if ranker times out.

Interviewer: Search?

Candidate: Inverted index of title, description, tags, channel, and transcripts. Indexer consumes video events. Sharded by token. Queries score relevance, popularity, freshness, channel authority. Eventually consistent — a new video is searchable within minutes. We may pre-index high-value channels faster.

Interviewer: Storage?

Candidate: Exabytes. Tiered. Derivatives are hot — heavy CDN backing. Originals can tier to cold storage after derivatives are stable, kept for re-transcoding if needed. Old videos with low view counts tier further. Reference counting on derivatives so unused ones can be culled. We may regenerate older derivatives in cheaper codecs (AV1) over time to save bandwidth.

Interviewer: Engagement counters?

Candidate: Same as the YouTube likes design. Sub-counter sharding per video to handle viral counts. Periodic recount from the per-user state for correctness. Display is eventually consistent; users tolerate seconds of lag.

Interviewer: Subscriptions?

Candidate: Channel has subscriber list. New upload event triggers notifications to subscribers via the notification system. Fan-out scales with subscriber count; celebrity channels may use hybrid push/pull like Twitter. The "Subscriptions" tab on the home page reads from per-user subscription state.

Interviewer: Multi-region?

Candidate: Derivatives geo-replicated for fast edge serving. Originals in upload region. Video metadata replicated; per-video home region for writes. CDN edges everywhere. Recommendation features per-region with cross-region trending shared. Geo-restrictions enforced at edge level.

Interviewer: Live streaming?

Candidate: Different pipeline. Ingest via RTMP or WebRTC; real-time transcode and HLS segment generation with low latency (LL-HLS, ~2-4 seconds glass-to-glass). Same CDN delivery model but cache TTLs are very short. DVR for recent content. Different from VOD because the manifest is constantly updated.

Interviewer: Summarize.

Candidate: Upload to object storage; async transcode farm produces multi-resolution derivatives. Multi-tier CDN serves with adaptive bitrate streaming via HLS/DASH. First-derivative publish makes videos visible quickly; higher resolutions land async. Recommendations are offline candidates plus online ranker. Search is async inverted index. Engagement counters sub-shard for viral content. Storage tiers from hot derivatives to cold originals. Multi-region uses CDN edges plus per-video home regions. The dominant cost is egress; CDN strategy and hit ratio drive the economics.

3. Practice Notes

Five-Minute Version

  1. Clarify: upload, watch, search, recommend.
  2. Invariant: durable storage; transcode; CDN delivery; sub-second start.
  3. Upload + per-segment parallel transcode.
  4. Multi-tier CDN; hit ratio above 90%.
  5. HLS/DASH adaptive bitrate; player-driven.
  6. Recommendations: offline + online ranker with timeout.
  7. Search: async inverted index.
  8. Sub-counter sharding for engagement; storage tiering.

One-Minute Closeout

YouTube is a video pipeline with a heavy CDN strategy. Upload writes original; transcode farm parallelizes per-segment to produce multi-resolution derivatives. Multi-tier CDN delivers with adaptive bitrate via HLS/DASH; hit ratio above 90% is what makes egress affordable. Recommendations are offline candidates plus online ranker with strict timeout. Search is async inverted index. Engagement counters sub-shard. Storage tiers from hot to cold. The dominant cost is egress; the dominant correctness concern is durable storage and visibility propagation.

Best Interview Phrases

  • "Transcoding parallelizes per-segment; manifest stitches it together for ABR."
  • "Multi-tier CDN with 90%+ hit ratio is what makes egress affordable."
  • "Adaptive bitrate is player-driven; the server just serves segments."
  • "Recommendations are offline candidates plus online ranker, with a hard timeout."
  • "Storage tiering from hot derivatives to cold originals is essential at exabyte scale."
