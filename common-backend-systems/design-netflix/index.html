1. Basic Knowledge And Solution

What This Question Is Testing

Netflix is curated, professionally-produced video streaming at planet scale. Unlike YouTube, the catalog is much smaller, but content is high-fidelity (4K HDR), pre-staged near every user, and recommendations drive most of the viewing. The hard part is the CDN strategy (Open Connect), adaptive bitrate, recommendations, and a binge-watching UX with seamless episode transitions.

The invariant is this:

Every cataloged title is available in every region where the user is licensed to watch; playback starts within a second, adapts to bandwidth, and resumes from the user's exact position; recommendations are personalized and refreshed; licensing rights are enforced at the playback boundary.

At ICT5 / L6+ level, the interviewer is testing whether you can design CDN pre-positioning (Open Connect appliances inside ISPs), encoding ladders, DRM, recommendation pipelines, A/B testing infrastructure, and reason about peak streaming during major launches.

60-Second Interview Framing

Spoken version:

I would design Netflix around four systems: a content ingestion + encoding pipeline that produces many quality levels and formats per title, a CDN — Netflix's Open Connect — that pre-positions content near every ISP, a playback service that authorizes and serves with DRM, and a recommendation engine that drives discovery. The interesting decisions are encoding ladders, ABR, DRM, CDN strategy, recommendation, and binge-watching UX.

Requirements To Clarify First

Functional requirements:

  • Users browse catalog by row (Trending, New Releases, For You).
  • Search titles.
  • Play video on web, mobile, smart TV, set-top.
  • Resume from last position.
  • Profiles (multiple per account).
  • Downloads for offline viewing.
  • Subtitles, multiple audio tracks.
  • Parental controls.

Non-functional requirements:

  • Sub-second playback start.
  • Adaptive bitrate up to 4K HDR.
  • High availability per region.
  • Strict DRM enforcement.
  • Regional content licensing.
  • Multi-device (4 profiles per account, configurable concurrent streams).
  • Off-peak: half of internet bandwidth in some regions.

Out of scope unless asked:

  • Live events (different pipeline).
  • Mobile games.
  • Production tools.

Content Pipeline

  1. Studio submits master file (high-bitrate, near-lossless).
  2. QC: automated + human review.
  3. Encoder generates ladder: dozens of variants per title. - Multiple resolutions (240p, 480p, 720p, 1080p, 4K). - Multiple codecs (H.264 widely supported; HEVC for 4K; AV1 newer). - HDR variants (HDR10, Dolby Vision). - Per-title optimization: choose bitrates appropriate to content complexity (a slow drama needs less than an action movie).
  4. Audio: multiple languages, formats (stereo, 5.1, Atmos).
  5. Subtitles: per language.
  6. Generate adaptive streaming manifests (HLS, DASH, MSS).
  7. Encrypt with DRM keys.
  8. Push to CDN.

Per-title encoding ladders: Netflix famously customizes bitrates per title. A talking-head documentary needs lower bitrates than an action movie to achieve the same perceptual quality. Saves bandwidth.

Interview phrase:

Per-title encoding ladders are Netflix's signature optimization. Why send 6 Mbps to a slow scene that looks great at 3 Mbps?

Open Connect — Netflix's CDN

Netflix runs its own CDN, with appliances installed inside ISPs and at internet exchanges.

  • Open Connect Appliances (OCAs) sit inside ISP networks.
  • Content is pre-positioned to OCAs based on predicted demand.
  • During off-peak, OCAs fetch new/predicted content from Netflix's origin.
  • During peak, OCAs serve directly to subscribers without crossing ISP boundary.

Benefits:

  • Massive bandwidth saved at ISP boundary.
  • Low latency to subscriber.
  • Cost: Netflix donates the hardware; ISPs love it because it reduces transit cost.

Pre-positioning algorithm:

  • Predict regional demand from recommendations, release schedules, historical patterns.
  • Push popular titles to all OCAs in the region.
  • Tail content stays at fewer OCAs; misses fall through to regional origins.

Adaptive Bitrate Streaming

Same as YouTube — HLS/DASH manifests with multiple bitrates; player picks based on bandwidth and buffer.

Netflix-specific:

  • Per-title ladders mean variant bitrates differ across titles.
  • Aggressive buffering on connected TVs (large memory).
  • Smart switching: avoid frequent up/down switches; minimize visible quality changes.

DRM

Required for studio content licensing.

  • Per-device DRM (Widevine for Android/Chrome; FairPlay for Apple; PlayReady for Windows).
  • Content encrypted with content key.
  • Playback service issues DRM license bound to device + session.
  • License has expiry; device must renew for long-running playback.
  • Offline downloads: license stored on device, expires.

Playback Flow

  1. User clicks Play.
  2. Client requests /playback/init with title_id and device.
  3. Playback service: - Authenticates user; checks rights for region. - Verifies device DRM capability. - Issues DRM license challenge. - Returns manifest URL pointed at nearest OCA.
  4. Player downloads manifest, picks initial bitrate.
  5. Player requests segments from OCA.
  6. Player decrypts using DRM key.
  7. Player heartbeats playback position to playhead service every ~30 seconds.
  8. On pause/exit, final position saved.

Sub-second start:

  • Manifest is small; fetched quickly.
  • Initial segments at modest bitrate to start fast.
  • Pre-fetch next segments while playing.

Recommendations

Two-stage (similar to YouTube):

  • Offline: daily build of candidate sets per profile from collaborative filtering, embedding similarity, popularity.
  • Online: ranker selects which rows and what order at request time.

Netflix is famous for A/B testing every UX decision; the recommender is constantly experimented on.

Personalization signals:

  • Watch history; completion rate.
  • Browsing behavior; hover time.
  • Rating (thumbs).
  • Genre affinities; cast preferences.
  • Time of day; device type.

Multi-Region Strategy

  • Open Connect appliances in every major ISP and IX.
  • Origin in cloud (AWS); a few regions.
  • Catalog and account services replicated globally.
  • Regional rights enforcement.

Failure Modes And Mitigations

OCA fails. Failover to peer OCA or regional origin. OCA health.
Encoder slow. Pre-queue popular titles weeks ahead. Encode queue depth.
DRM service down. Failover; cached licenses for active sessions. DRM service availability.
Major launch surge. Pre-position; auto-scale streams. Per-title concurrent stream count.
Network bottleneck at ISP. OCA serves locally; bypass transit. ISP egress vs internal serve ratio.
Bad encoding artifact. QC catches; manual review. Reported playback issues.
Geographic rights violation. Strict region check; VPN detection. License violation reports.
Player stuck buffering. ABR drops; alternative variants. Rebuffer rate.
Account sharing abuse. Concurrent stream limits; recent IP. Profile usage anomalies.
Cross-region failover. Active-active for cloud control plane. Cloud service availability.

Operational Metrics

Correctness:

  • Playback start failure rate.
  • Rebuffer ratio.
  • License denial rate.
  • Catalog inconsistency between regions.

Capacity:

  • OCA fill rates; cache hit ratio at OCA.
  • Concurrent streams by region.
  • Encoder throughput.

Business:

  • DAU; hours watched.
  • Completion rate per title.
  • Discovery → play conversion.
  • Churn rate.

Tradeoffs To Call Out

  • Per-title encoding ladders save bandwidth but add encoding complexity.
  • Pre-positioning at OCAs costs upfront but saves enormously at peak.
  • Strict DRM is mandatory for licensing but adds player complexity.
  • Aggressive personalization vs deterministic UX.
  • Higher bitrates please users but increase egress cost.
  • Offline downloads consume storage and require DRM-bound playback.

Generic CDN. Netflix's Open Connect: ISP-embedded appliances.

2. Simulated Interview

Interviewer: Design Netflix.

Candidate: Four core systems: content ingestion + encoding pipeline, Open Connect CDN with ISP appliances, playback service with DRM, and recommendation engine. The invariant is sub-second playback start, adaptive quality, per-region licensing, and personalized discovery. Heavy emphasis on Open Connect because egress cost dominates.

Interviewer: Walk me through the content pipeline.

Candidate: Studio submits a master file. QC checks artifacts. Encoder generates a per-title ladder: dozens of variants combining resolutions (240p through 4K), codecs (H.264, HEVC, AV1), HDR formats. Per-title means the bitrates are tuned to the content — a slow drama uses less than an action movie. Multiple audio tracks per language, subtitles, accessibility tracks. Generate HLS/DASH manifests. Encrypt with DRM. Push to Open Connect.

Interviewer: Open Connect — what's special?

Candidate: Netflix runs its own CDN. Open Connect Appliances are physical servers Netflix gives to ISPs to install in their networks. During off-peak (3 AM), OCAs pull content from Netflix's origin. During peak, OCAs serve subscribers directly without traffic crossing the ISP boundary. The ISP saves transit cost; Netflix saves CDN cost; subscribers get low latency. Open Connect serves the vast majority of Netflix traffic.

Interviewer: How does it pre-position?

Candidate: Predict regional demand using recommendation outputs, release schedules, historical patterns. Push predicted-popular titles to all OCAs in the region. Tail content goes to fewer OCAs; misses fall through to regional cloud origin. The prediction loop is continuous; new releases get pushed aggressively before launch.

Interviewer: Walk me through a play.

Candidate: User clicks Play. Client calls playback/init. Playback service authenticates user, checks regional rights (is this title licensed in the user's country?), issues a DRM license challenge bound to the device, returns the manifest URL pointed at the nearest OCA. Player downloads manifest, decrypts segments using DRM, starts with a moderate bitrate to begin fast, then adaptively switches based on bandwidth. Player heartbeats playhead every 30 seconds.

Interviewer: DRM?

Candidate: Per-platform: Widevine (Android/Chrome), FairPlay (Apple), PlayReady (Windows). Content is encrypted with a content key. Playback service issues a DRM license that includes the content key, bound to the device and session. The player provides the license to its platform DRM module which decrypts segments without exposing the key. For offline downloads, the license is stored on-device with an expiry.

Interviewer: Recommendations?

Candidate: Two-stage. Offline: daily batch builds candidate sets per profile from collaborative filtering, embedding similarity, popularity. Each profile has stored candidate rows ("Continue Watching," "Trending Now," "Because You Watched X," etc.). Online: when user opens the app, the recommender selects which rows to show and what order, plus per-row ordering of titles. Heavy A/B testing on UI and ranking.

Interviewer: Per-title encoding ladders?

Candidate: Instead of one universal ladder (e.g., 1080p at 6 Mbps for every title), Netflix optimizes per title. A talking-head doc can look great at 3 Mbps 1080p; an action movie needs 6+ Mbps. By tuning per content, average bitrate drops significantly, saving CDN cost without quality loss. Encoder runs perceptual quality metrics to find the optimal point.

Interviewer: A major launch — Stranger Things season finale.

Candidate: Pre-position aggressively to all OCAs days before. Predict the surge and stage capacity. Recommendation engine surfaces it prominently. On launch night, OCAs are pre-filled; ISPs see local serving; cloud origin sees minimal traffic. Auto-scale playback service capacity. Monitor rebuffer rates and adjust.

Interviewer: Profile playhead — user watches on TV, picks up on phone.

Candidate: Per-profile per-title playhead synced via the playhead service. Heartbeat from TV during playback updates server-side. When user opens phone, "Continue Watching" row shows the last position. Server-side state is authoritative; client may have a cached recent value.

Interviewer: Search?

Candidate: Separate search service over inverted index of titles, casts, genres, descriptions. Async indexing. Sub-second queries. Personalization layer ranks results by viewer preference.

Interviewer: What about live events (NFL, Tyson fight)?

Candidate: Different pipeline. Real-time ingest from production trucks; live encoder produces adaptive bitrates with low latency; HLS/DASH with short segments (~2 seconds). Open Connect serves live segments globally. Latency target: ~10-15 seconds glass-to-glass for traditional, lower for premium. DVR for time-shifting.

Interviewer: Summarize.

Candidate: Content pipeline produces per-title encoding ladders with DRM. Open Connect — ISP-embedded appliances — serves the bulk of streams locally. Playback service authorizes per session; DRM gates content. Two-stage recommendations drive discovery. Per-profile playhead syncs across devices. A/B testing pervades the experience. The dominant cost is egress; Open Connect is the architectural answer. Per-title encoding is the optimization that saves a fortune.

Netflix is curated streaming with Open Connect as the architectural centerpiece. ISP-embedded OCAs pre-position content during off-peak and serve subscribers locally during peak, keeping traffic off the transit. Per-title encoding ladders save bandwidth without quality loss. DRM enforces licensing. Two-stage recommendations drive discovery. Per-profile playheads sync across devices. The dominant cost is egress; Open Connect is the answer.
